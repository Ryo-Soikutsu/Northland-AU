# Overview
The Quick-Response Fleet (QRF) doctrine outlines the guidelines and specifications for fleet composition, individual ship outfitting as well as operational tactics, techniques and procedures (TTPs).

This doctrine is intended for the patrol of Foundation-controlled spaces, as well as conducting ad-hoc operations such as armed convoy or search-and-rescue (S&R). QRF doctrines vary between operation areas, but are generally separated into regular operations and abyssal operations. A typical squad running this doctrine will have between 5-8 ships, up to 15 ships if wartime protocols are activated. 

# Fleet Composition
The QRF doctrine is made up of 4 to 5 primary ship classes
- Interceptors - Lightly-armored and agile fighters, modified to have incredible sub-light and FTL speeds and accelerations, and advanced ECM and disruption technology. Typically only fitted with CIWS turrets to shoot down incoming missiles and projectiles. In certain configurations, interceptors may incorporate specialized propulsion systems enabling near-instantaneous FTL transition, significantly reducing time-to-target during response operations
	- During wartime protocols, interceptors may be reinforced with specialized heavy fighters or electronic attack ships to suppress hostile targets and prevent escape attempts
- Destroyers - The primary damage-dealing vessels of the fleet, typically equipped with short- to medium-range primary weapon systems supported by point-defense turrets for interception of missiles, drones, and light fighter craft. These ships form the core offensive element of QRF engagements, providing sustained firepower and tactical pressure against medium-class targets
- Heavy assault cruisers (HAC) - Cruiser-class heavy combat vessels deployed primarily under wartime conditions where significant hostile resistance is expected. HACs carry enhanced short- to medium-range armament packages and may, in rare cases, be fitted with anti-capital weapon systems such as entropic disintegrators. They may also incorporate specialized damage control and survivability modules that allow the ship's hull and armor to sustain significantly increased amounts of damage for a short period of time
- Command ship - Mobile command-and-control platforms, typically operating in the form of light or support carriers during peacetime or routine operations. These vessels function as fleet coordination hubs, maintaining communications with higher-echelon command networks while directing QRF assets in real time
	- During wartime protocols, standard command ships may be replaced with their assault carrier variants, with increased survivability, offensive capabilities, and operational endurance while serving as the fleet’s primary command node

This doctrine also supports the use of specialized ship classes, which are used during ad-hoc operations or special deployments
- Rescue craft - Frigate-sized vessels dedicated to medical evacuation, search-and-rescue (S&R), and casualty recovery operations. These ships are equipped with advanced medical facilities, high-resolution sensor suites, and redundant communications relay systems. They are typically unarmed and rely on escort protection, compensating with reinforced shielding and structural resilience
- Tugboats - Lightly armored frigate-class utility vessels designed for towing, recovery, and repositioning operations. Equipped with high-output propulsion systems and either mechanical winch assemblies or advanced magnetic coupling systems, they are capable of maneuvering significantly larger vessels or debris fields. Tugboats are generally unarmed and require escort support, with reduced armor and shielding prioritized in favor of maximum thrust efficiency and operational endurance
- Logistics tender - Light carrier–class support vessels functioning as mobile repair, resupply, and rearm platforms for extended QRF deployments. These ships carry extensive stores of fuel, oxygen, munitions, and replacement components or raw materials, alongside onboard fabrication and assembly systems, including automated molecular disassembly-reassembly arrays and nanobot-assisted remote repair infrastructure 
	- Can be used as a substitute for a rescue craft if a more durable ship is required, and will usually be paired together with one or more rescue craft for large scale S&R missions, serving as a medical resupply and auxiliary patient storage
	- During wartime protocols, at least one logistics tender will be assigned per QRF deployment to serve as a mobile repair and refuel platform, and backup communications relay platform

# Outfitting
Most vessels within this doctrine are fitted with standardized abyssal-resistant shielding systems and light composite armor optimized for long-duration environmental exposure and operational endurance. In wartime configurations, higher-value platforms such as specialized role ships and command vessels are upgraded with heavy armor suites, trading maneuverability and acceleration performance for significantly increased survivability under sustained fire.

Interceptor craft and fighter-class vessels typically rely on electronic warfare survivability rather than structural protection. These platforms are equipped with electromagnetic countermeasures (ECM) designed to degrade hostile targeting solutions, disrupt weapons tracking, interfere with missile guidance systems, and in some cases impede warp or jump drive acquisition locks and communications integrity. They also deploy expendable countermeasure packages such as chaff and flares to defeat thermal and sensor-based tracking systems. Interceptors, in particular, prioritize extreme agility and velocity over durability, accepting minimal physical protection in exchange for evasive performance envelopes that reduce the probability of successful target acquisition.

Command ships and other mission-critical platforms are reinforced with layered defensive architectures. These commonly include redundant shield generators, multi-layer or ablative armor configurations, and high-output ECM suites optimized for breaking sustained tracking and targeting solutions under contested conditions. They also incorporate independent backup power systems to maintain essential operations in the event of primary reactor disruption or localized system failure.

At the upper end of defensive capability, larger capital or command vessels may integrate active protection systems (APS), which generate localized energy fields or interception barriers intended to disrupt, deflect, or absorb incoming projectiles and energy strikes before impact. These systems are highly energy-intensive and are therefore typically reserved for vessels with substantial power generation capacity and strict survivability requirements.

## Offensive Subsystems
Specialized vessel classes such as logistics tenders and tugboats are generally not equipped with dedicated offensive weapon systems, and therefore rely on escort craft for protection during both transit and operational deployment. Depending on mission requirements and expected threat levels, these ships may still be fitted with close-in weapon systems (CIWS) for point-defense purposes.

CIWS platforms are fielded in several configurations, with the most common being laser-based, missile-based, and kinetic-based systems. Laser-based CIWS are the standard defensive solution, utilizing sustained high-energy beams to rapidly heat and vaporize incoming threats. Some laser CIWS employ a gatling-style configuration, replacing conventional ballistic ammunition with concentrated laser pulses or “laser darts” to achieve higher tracking and interception rates. Missile-based CIWS are less frequently deployed, typically reserved for engaging larger or more resilient projectiles that cannot be neutralized quickly enough by laser or kinetic systems. Kinetic-based CIWS are comparatively rare, but remain effective against dense swarms of fragile targets such as micro-missiles, using high-volume flechette dispersal to saturate an interception zone.

Dedicated combat vessels are equipped with primary ship-to-ship weapon systems such as plasma cannons, missile batteries, and assault cannons — heavy autocannon platforms designed to penetrate multiple layers of reinforced armor. In addition to CIWS, ships may also mount secondary weapon systems including auto-targeting missile launchers and high-tracking railguns optimized for precision strikes against critical subsystems such as propulsion, sensors, or weapon mounts. When engagements against larger hostile vessels are anticipated, ships may instead be outfitted with anti-capital weaponry, including abyssal entropic disintegrators, fixed heavy artillery cannons, and long-range cruise missile batteries intended for sustained stand-off engagements.

### Command Ships
Under standard operating conditions, command ships are equipped with comparatively modest ship-to-ship armaments, prioritizing fleet coordination and survivability over direct offensive output. Defensive coverage is primarily provided through distributed close-in weapon systems (CIWS) positioned across the hull. Typical loadouts include multi-barrel autocannons and compact missile cells, supplemented by enhanced laser-based gatling CIWS variants optimized for rapid interception of lightly armored, high-velocity threats and small craft. In assault carrier configurations, these platforms may carry significantly heavier weaponry, including large-caliber autocannons, expanded missile arrays, or railgun systems, selected according to anticipated threat profiles.

A defining capability of command ships is their role as unmanned aerial and combat drone control hubs. These vessels deploy, coordinate, and sustain large-scale swarms of autonomous fighter drones, which serve multiple tactical and offensive functions: screening high-value fleet elements from enemy attacks, saturating and degrading enemy sensor and tracking systems, and executing strafing run attacks against medium and large targets. The drone complement typically spans a broad spectrum of roles, from light space-superiority interceptors to heavy ordnance-carrying bomber drones designed for capital-scale engagements.

To sustain prolonged operations, command ships are equipped with limited onboard fabrication capabilities, allowing for partial replenishment and repair of drone assets using stored raw materials and components. However, for extended engagements or high attrition scenarios, they remain dependent on logistics tenders to resupply materials, replace specialized parts, and maintain sustained sortie generation rates.
