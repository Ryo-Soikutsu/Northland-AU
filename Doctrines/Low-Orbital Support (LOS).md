# Overview
The Low-Orbital Support (LOS) doctrine outlines the guidelines and specifications for fleet composition, individual ship outfitting as well as operational tactics, techniques and procedures (TTPs).

This doctrine is focused around the support of ground operations, such as evacuations, base defense and strategic offensive missions. A typical squad running this doctrine will contain anywhere from 5-7 ships, with full LOS fleets capable of having as many as 15-20 squads at once. Ships in this doctrine should be rated for low orbit flight, with altitudes ranging from 25-100km for primary ground support vessels, to 250-1000km for escorts and logistics depending on planetary and atmospheric conditions.

# Composition
The LOS doctrine is made up of three primary ship classes:
- **Ground support**: The core of the doctrine, these ships provide orbital firepower, command and control and manpower. These ships also feature airstrips for Pelican-class and Eagle-class aircraft to take off and land. These crafts typically operate at sub-orbital altitudes of 25-100km
- **Heavy logistics**: Small, heavily armored and lightly armed freighters, these ships carry drop pods and freight containers containing equipment needed to support ground operations. These ships typically operate around altitudes of 350-1000km
- **Escorts**: Nimble and heavily armed vessels, these ships primarily help defend the rest of the doctrine from hostile ships. Typically, light to medium drone carriers take on this role, but modifications of this doctrine exist where fast destroyers and cruisers are used instead. These ships typically orbit around altitudes of 250-500km

This doctrine includes three dedicated aircraft classes:
- **Pelican-class freight jet:** A medium transport aircraft built around a modular fuselage system, allowing rapid reconfiguration between an armored personnel carrier, aerial fuel tanker, and heavy cargo transporter. For limited self-defense during loading and extraction operations, it is equipped with twin chin-mounted auto-cannons and manually operated door-mounted machine guns for close-range defensive fire support.
- **Eagle-class close air support fighter:** A heavily armored, relatively low-speed strike aircraft optimized for direct close air support in active combat zones. It also performs secondary battlefield reconnaissance duties, identifying threats ahead of advancing forces. Its weapon hardpoints are reconfigurable on the ground via support infrastructure prior to deployment, but cannot be altered mid-mission due to maintenance and turnaround constraints.
- **Raptor-class air superiority fighter:** A lightweight, highly maneuverable interceptor designed to secure and maintain control of contested airspace over operational theaters. It is typically reserved for scenarios where orbital or suborbital air assets are not available or have not yet been established, as those platforms fulfill similar roles more effectively. Raptors are instead most commonly deployed from forward airbases once infrastructure is in place, where their rapid response capability and low replacement cost make them an efficient supplement to higher-end aerospace systems.

Other air and sea-based craft, infantry support vehicles and equipment and military infrastructure can be easily incorporated into this doctrine, and should be considered on a case-by-case basis.

# Firepower and Weaponry
Heavy logistics vessels typically carry light point defense weaponry to defend against drones and missiles, as well as traditional countermeasures such as decoys and area-of-effect EMP generators. Point defense systems can range from laser-based close-in weapons systems (CIWS) for small and fast-moving projectiles to short-range high-tracking auto-cannons for heavier missiles and small interceptor ships.

Escort ships tend to be armed with medium to heavy cannons, designed for ship-to-ship combat. These can range from heavy railguns to plasma cannons, which can tear through a ship's armor with relative ease. For Foundation-led operations, drone carriers tend to be used instead due to the lower manpower requirements, which rely on light and medium drone fighter squadrons to swarm and overwhelm a target's defense systems. These carriers may also be equipped with heavy weaponry as supplemental damage if fighter squadrons are used to distract and defend the carrier.

Ground support ships typically do not have any ship-to-ship weapons, only being armed with laser-based point defense weaponry for stray missiles and/or drones. Instead, they are outfitted with a range of suborbital/low-orbit weaponry, which can be swapped around between deployments. Firepower options can range from traditional explosive bombardments and barrages, to single high-power strikes and even environmental manipulation.

## Offensive Attacks
- Artillery Barrage - Prolonged bombardment of artillery shells over a wide area, can be loaded with a variety of shells for different roles
	- APFSDS - Non-explosive, kinetic penetration rounds designed to counter tanks, mechs and other heavily-armored vehicles. Primarily made of tungsten or depleted uranium
	- High-Explosive - Anti-infantry rounds with moderate blast and low fragmentation, designed to counter low to medium armored targets and infantry
	- Flechette/Fragmentation - Airburst rounds containing hundreds of micro-darts or custom-designed casings design to shatter into numerous shrapnel pieces, extremely powerful against infantry with high potential collateral damage
	- Incendiary/Thermobaric - Special rounds loaded with fuel instead of regular explosives, creates massive fuel clouds when detonated, causing large fires in the area of impacts. An effective area-denial weapon, designed to destroy infantry-fortified positions with ease
- Cluster-Dispersion Smart Shrapnel (CDSS) Airburst - A single, large carrier missile containing hundreds to thousands of micro-drones. When the missile is detonated, the drones are released and lock onto heat signatures, mechanical vibrations or energy sources, before detonating tiny explosives to the target when in range
- Electromagnetic Strike - Underslung railcannon which launches depleted uranium-core APFSDS rounds with high accuracy, primarily designed for anti-tank and anti-building purposes. Fires in three-round bursts, allowing complete penetration of virtually any bunker or fortification
- Missile Barrage - Prolonged firing of cluster missiles over a wide area targeting aerial forces. Primarily designed for aerial suppression of air superiority fighters and other aircraft when anti-air weaponry are disabled or insufficient

## Non-Lethal Attacks
- EMP Strike - Underslung railcannon which launches plasma-filled rounds (aka 'plasma slugs'), detonates on impact with little destructive force but a large, localized EMP effect. Effectively disables technology and other advanced equipment in the locale, while dealing minimal harm to humans and other organic beings
- Biochemical Storm - Multiple cluster missiles that detonate when close to the ground, releasing gas or toxins over a large area. Primarily meant to knock out, confuse or otherwise disable hostile forces without dealing significant damage. Effective in evacuations and other defensive operations that require area denial with little to no civilian/friendly casualty

## Broken Arrow Protocol
Declares that friendly forces have been overwhelmed and that mission-critical objectives are at imminent risk of hostile capture. Authorization of a Broken Arrow (BA) strike permits the deployment of maximum-force orbital bombardment measures intended to inflict catastrophic losses on hostile forces and deny the objective entirely, with minimal consideration for the survival of remaining friendly personnel and civilians, or preservation of infrastructure and equipment.

To activate a Broken Arrow Protocol, pre-authorization must be approved by the Executive Committee prior to the strike or mission, which acknowledges the potential collateral damage and operation failure, and authorizes the additional allocation of necessary resources required to perform the strike. When a Broken Arrow strike is requested, the weapons master computer will require the authorization codes of the commanding weapon systems officer (WSO), the operation or mission commander and the ship master. 

The weapon systems and munitions authorized for a Broken Arrow strike vary depending on operational requirements and mission parameters. In most deployments, Broken Arrow bombardments are conducted using existing armaments mounted aboard orbital or suborbital support craft. In rare cases, however, specialized Broken Arrow strike platforms are deployed specifically for large-scale saturation or denial bombardments.

Due to the extreme firing rates, sustained power draw, and thermal loads involved, execution of a Broken Arrow strike typically places severe mechanical stress on the weapon assemblies, reactor systems, and power distribution infrastructure of the participating craft. As a result, offensive weapon systems are often rendered partially or fully inoperable following the strike until extensive maintenance and repairs can be conducted, either in-field or at a properly equipped outpost or support facility.
